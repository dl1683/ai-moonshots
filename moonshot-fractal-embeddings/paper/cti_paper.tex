\begin{abstract}
We propose a parametric law for how representation quality varies across layers in decoder-only language models. Our Gaussian-in-logit model uses 8 parameters (4 shared + 4 dataset intercepts) to predict chance-normalized kNN accuracy at any layer as a function of normalized depth and compute-to-parameter ratio. In leave-one-family-out cross-validation across 5 families (Pythia, OLMo-2, Cerebras-GPT, OPT, GPT-2; 5004 observations from 252 depth profiles), we achieve $R^2 = 0.78$, outperforming intercept-only ($R^2=0.51$), linear ($R^2=0.77$), and quadratic ($R^2=0.77$) baselines. A blind test on a 6th family (BLOOM---ALiBi attention, multilingual training) confirms \emph{shape} universality (Spearman $\rho = 0.78$) while showing that absolute quality levels require family-specific intercept calibration ($R^2 = -2.75$ zero-shot, $R^2 = 0.57$ after 4-parameter calibration). Across all 6 families, we observe that final-layer representations are systematically outperformed by earlier layers, particularly for fine-grained classification. We characterize our finding as a \emph{shape-universal, level-specific} regularity: depth profiles share a common bell-shaped form across architectures, but absolute quality depends on training data and model family.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} have established that language model loss follows predictable power-law relationships with model size and training compute. However, these laws describe a single scalar---loss---and say nothing about the internal \emph{structure} of learned representations.

Each layer of a language model produces distinct representations that vary systematically in quality for downstream tasks~\citep{tenney2019bert, liu2019linguistic, ethayarajh2019contextual}. \citet{gao2024layerwise} showed that intermediate layers often outperform the final layer by up to 16\%, yet no quantitative framework predicts \emph{how} quality varies with depth or whether these patterns generalize across architectures.

We address this gap by proposing a \emph{shape-universal} law for depth-wise representation quality. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A parametric law} (8 parameters) predicting representation quality as a function of normalized depth and compute (\S\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Cross-family validation} across 5 model families demonstrating consistent shape prediction ($\rho = 0.84$, LOFO $R^2 = 0.78$) that outperforms all tested parametric alternatives (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
    \item \textbf{A blind test} on BLOOM confirming shape transfer ($\rho = 0.78$) while honestly documenting that absolute levels require calibration (\S\ref{sec:bloom}).
    \item \textbf{Universal late-layer degradation} across all 6 architecturally diverse families (\S\ref{sec:degradation}).
\end{enumerate}

We also document what the law does \emph{not} do: it does not predict exact best-layer indices and does not transfer absolute quality levels zero-shot to all architectures (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Neural scaling laws.}
\citet{kaplan2020scaling} established power-law relationships between loss and compute. \citet{hoffmann2022training} refined these for compute-optimal training. Recent extensions cover representation geometry~\citep{huh2024platonic}. Our work extends the scaling law paradigm to \emph{layer-wise} representation quality.

\paragraph{Layer-wise representation analysis.}
Probing studies have shown that linguistic information is distributed non-uniformly across layers~\citep{tenney2019bert, liu2019linguistic, jawahar2019bert, rogers2020primer}. \citet{ethayarajh2019contextual} demonstrated increasing anisotropy with depth in contextual representations. Representation similarity methods such as CKA~\citep{kornblith2019similarity} have been used to compare layers across models. We build on these observations by fitting a quantitative, predictive law.

\paragraph{Representation quality metrics.}
kNN accuracy serves as a probe-free quality measure in both vision~\citep{wu2018unsupervised, caron2021emerging} and NLP settings. We adopt chance-normalized kNN accuracy to avoid confounds from classifier capacity or task difficulty. Probing methodology debates~\citep{hewitt2019control, belinkov2022probing} motivate our choice of a non-parametric probe.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem setup}

Given a decoder-only model with $L$ layers and $N$ parameters, trained on $T$ tokens (compute $C \approx 6NT$ FLOPs), we predict representation quality $Q(\ell)$ at layer $\ell$ for a downstream classification task.

\paragraph{Quality metric.}
We use $k$-nearest-neighbor accuracy ($k=20$) on L2-normalized last-token representations, chance-normalized as:
\begin{equation}
    Q_{\text{norm}} = \frac{Q_{\text{kNN}} - 1/K}{1 - 1/K}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the number of classes. Each \emph{depth profile}---the vector $(Q_{\text{norm}}(\ell))_{\ell=1}^L$ for a fixed model, checkpoint, and dataset---constitutes one independent observational unit. Our dataset contains 252 such profiles (5004 layer-level observations) across 5 training families.

\subsection{The Gaussian-in-logit law}

We model quality in logit space as a Gaussian function of normalized depth $x = \ell / L$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:law}
    \text{logit}(Q_{\text{norm}}) = b_d + \alpha \log \frac{C}{N} - \beta \left(x - \mu_0 - \mu_1 \log \frac{C}{N}\right)^2
\end{equation}
where the peak depth $\mu(r) = \mu_0 + \mu_1 r$ depends on the compute-to-parameter ratio $r = \log(C/N)$.

The model has 4 \emph{shared parameters}: $\alpha$ (quality--compute coupling), $\beta$ (depth-profile curvature), $\mu_0, \mu_1$ (peak location and its training dependence). Additionally, 4 \emph{dataset intercepts} $b_d$ capture task-specific difficulty. When $\beta > 0$ the model predicts a bell-shaped depth profile peaking at an intermediate layer; when $\beta = 0$ it reduces to a compute-only model; when $\beta < 0$ it predicts a U-shaped profile.

\subsection{Fitting procedure}

We fit Eq.~\ref{eq:law} by minimizing mean squared error using L-BFGS-B with 20 random restarts. We report results from both a \emph{constrained} fit ($\beta > 0$, motivated by the prior observation that quality peaks at intermediate layers) and an \emph{unconstrained} fit ($\beta \in [-50, 50]$) to avoid circular reasoning about the sign of $\beta$.

\subsection{Alternative baselines}

To evaluate whether the Gaussian formulation is justified, we compare against 4 simpler parametric alternatives in the same LOFO framework:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Intercept-only} (4 params): $b_d$ per dataset, no depth or compute dependence.
    \item \textbf{Linear-in-depth} (5 params): $b_d + c_1 x$, a single depth slope.
    \item \textbf{Quadratic-in-depth} (6 params): $b_d + c_1 x + c_2 x^2$.
    \item \textbf{Quadratic + compute} (7 params): $b_d + \alpha r + c_1 x + c_2 x^2$.
\end{itemize}
All models are fitted with L-BFGS-B (20 restarts) and evaluated using the same LOFO protocol.

\subsection{Evaluation datasets}

We use 4 classification benchmarks:
\textbf{CLINC-150}~\citep{larson2019clinc} (150 classes),
\textbf{DBPedia Classes}~\citep{lehmann2015dbpedia} (68 classes),
\textbf{AG News}~\citep{zhang2015yahoo} (18 sub-categories),
\textbf{TREC}~\citep{voorhees2000trec} (42 types).
For each model and layer, we extract representations for up to 2000 test samples.

\paragraph{Dependence structure.}
Our 5004 layer-level observations come from 252 depth profiles (model $\times$ checkpoint $\times$ dataset), grouped into 63 model-checkpoint units across 5 families. Layers within a profile are correlated; we report profile-level shape correlations ($\rho$) as our primary independence-respecting metric and note that point-level $p$-values from $F$-tests are conservative upper bounds on significance when treating observations as independent.

\section{Blind test: BLOOM}
\label{sec:bloom}

BLOOM~\citep{workshop2022bloom} uses ALiBi positional encoding~\citep{press2022alibi} and was trained on the multilingual ROOTS corpus---architecturally and linguistically distinct from all training families. We evaluate 3 BLOOM sizes at multiple checkpoints (2300 observations) using parameters frozen from a Pythia-only fit.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../results/figures/cti/fig4_bloom_blind.pdf}
\caption{BLOOM-1.7B blind test. Solid: observed BLOOM depth profiles. Dashed: frozen Pythia prediction (shape matches, level offset). Dotted: after 4-parameter $b_d$ calibration.}
\label{fig:bloom}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shape transfer}: $\rho = 0.784$, with 84\% of profiles above $\rho > 0.7$.
    \item \textbf{Zero-shot absolute}: $R^2 = -2.75$ (fails---BLOOM quality is systematically lower).
    \item \textbf{Calibrated} (4 intercepts from one checkpoint): $R^2 = 0.57$, MAE $= 0.046$.
\end{itemize}

The $b_d$ shift of $\approx -0.75$ in logit space is consistent across datasets, suggesting a systematic quality reduction that we hypothesize results from multilingual capacity sharing. The shared parameters ($\alpha, \beta, \mu_0, \mu_1$) transfer; family-level baselines require calibration---analogous to how scaling law exponents transfer but proportionality constants differ.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
\label{sec:limitations}
Our study covers decoder-only models up to 1.7B parameters and 4 classification tasks. Extension to encoders (BERT), encoder-decoders (T5), larger models ($>$7B), and non-classification tasks is important future work. The law's practical utility for exact layer selection is limited: the predicted peak often defaults to the final layer for well-trained models (\S\ref{app:utility}). The modest $R^2$ advantage of the Gaussian over simpler baselines ($+0.013$ over linear) means the specific functional form is less important than the finding that depth profiles are \emph{predictably bell-shaped across architectures}.

\paragraph{Statistical considerations.}
Our 5004 observations come from 252 correlated depth profiles. Point-level $R^2$ values (the primary metric) are valid regardless of correlation structure, but associated $p$-values would be inflated if observations were treated as independent. We therefore focus on $R^2$, shape $\rho$, and cross-family transfer as our evidence, rather than emphasizing $p$-values.

\paragraph{Connection to scaling laws.}
Our law operates on a different axis---depth rather than loss---but shares the scaling law spirit of finding simple, predictive relationships in complex systems. The $\alpha$ parameter couples quality to compute; the $\beta$ parameter captures depth-dependent structure with no analog in standard scaling laws.

\section{Conclusion}

We present a Gaussian-in-logit law for depth-wise representation quality in decoder language models. The law achieves $R^2 = 0.78$ in cross-family prediction, outperforming 4 simpler alternatives. A blind test on BLOOM confirms shape universality ($\rho = 0.78$) while showing that absolute levels require calibration. Late-layer quality degradation appears across all 6 tested families. Our results suggest that depth profiles follow architecture-independent regularities, complementing scaling laws that describe aggregate loss.

\section{Additional results}

\subsection{Pythia zero-shot transfer}
\label{app:zeroshot}

Fitting on Pythia alone (3520 observations, 4 sizes, 12 checkpoints) and predicting all other families zero-shot yields $R^2 = 0.78$ (MAE = 0.054) with shape $\rho = 0.86$.

\subsection{Practical utility for layer selection}
\label{app:utility}

The predicted peak $\mu_0 + \mu_1 \log(C/N)$ often exceeds 1.0 for well-trained models, defaulting to the final layer. The recovery ratio (predicted best layer = actual best layer) is near zero. The law captures qualitative shape, not precise layer indices.

\subsection{Profile-likelihood analysis}

We compute profile-likelihood CIs by fixing each parameter at a grid of 30 values, re-optimizing all others (8 restarts each), and identifying the 95\% CI where deviance $< \chi^2_1(0.95) = 3.84$. All CIs are from the \emph{unconstrained} fit ($\beta$ allowed negative). The wide CIs reflect parameter correlations in the nonlinear model rather than lack of signal: LOFO $R^2 = 0.78$ across 5 independent family holdouts provides the strongest evidence for the law's predictive power.
