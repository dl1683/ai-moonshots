\begin{abstract}
We discover a low-dimensional universal law governing how representation quality varies across layers and training compute in decoder-only language models. Our law, $\text{logit}(Q) = b_d + \alpha \log(C/N) - \beta(x - \mu(\log C/N))^2$, uses just 8 parameters to predict chance-normalized kNN accuracy $Q$ at normalized depth $x = \ell/L$ as a function of compute-to-parameter ratio $C/N$. Through leave-one-family-out cross-validation across 5 model families (Pythia, OLMo-2, Cerebras-GPT, OPT, GPT-2), we achieve $R^2 = 0.78$ with all folds significant at $p < 10^{-16}$. A blind test on a 6th family (BLOOM, with ALiBi attention and multilingual training) confirms universal shape ($\rho = 0.78$) while revealing that absolute quality level requires minimal family calibration (4 dataset intercepts). We document universal late-training quality degradation across all 6 architecturally diverse families: the final layer is systematically outperformed by earlier layers, particularly for fine-grained classification tasks. Our findings suggest that depth-wise representation geometry follows architecture-independent regularities analogous to scaling laws, with practical implications for layer selection in representation extraction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} have revealed that loss follows predictable power-law relationships with model size and training compute. These laws have proven invaluable for predicting model performance and allocating training budgets. However, scaling laws describe a single scalar (loss) and say nothing about the internal \emph{structure} of learned representations.

Modern language models are not monolithic: each layer produces distinct representations that vary systematically in quality~\citep{tenney2019bert, liu2019linguistic}. Recent work has shown that intermediate layers often outperform the final layer for downstream tasks~\citep{gao2024scaling}, yet no quantitative framework exists for predicting \emph{which} layer produces the best representations, \emph{how} quality varies with depth, or whether these patterns generalize across architectures.

We address this gap by proposing a universal law for depth-wise representation quality. Our key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A parametric law} relating representation quality to normalized depth, compute, and parameters via an 8-parameter Gaussian-in-logit model (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Cross-family validation} across 6 model families (16 models, $>$5000 observations) demonstrating shape universality ($\rho = 0.78$--$0.90$) and strong predictive accuracy (LOFO $R^2 = 0.78$) with all $p < 10^{-16}$ (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
    \item \textbf{A blind prediction test} on BLOOM (architecturally distinct: ALiBi attention, multilingual training), confirming shape universality but revealing that absolute quality level requires per-family calibration (Section~\ref{sec:bloom}).
    \item \textbf{Universal late-training degradation}: all 6 families show final-layer quality below peak, with GPT-2 showing up to 0.21 gap (Section~\ref{sec:degradation}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Neural Scaling Laws.}
\citet{kaplan2020scaling} established power-law relationships between loss, model size, and data. \citet{hoffmann2022training} refined these for compute-optimal training. Recent work extends scaling laws to downstream tasks~\citep{isik2024scaling}, emergent abilities~\citep{wei2022emergent}, and representation geometry~\citep{huh2024platonic}. Our work differs by modeling \emph{layer-wise} quality profiles rather than aggregate metrics.

\paragraph{Probing and Layer Selection.}
The NLP probing literature documents that linguistic information is distributed non-uniformly across layers~\citep{tenney2019bert, liu2019linguistic, belinkov2022probing}. \citet{gao2024scaling} showed that mid-layer representations outperform final-layer ones by up to 16\%. We formalize these observations into a predictive law.

\paragraph{Representation Quality Metrics.}
kNN accuracy has been used as a probe-free measure of representation quality~\citep{wu2018unsupervised, caron2021emerging}. We adopt chance-normalized kNN accuracy as our primary metric, avoiding confounds from classifier capacity.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Setup}

Given a decoder-only language model with $L$ layers and $N$ parameters, trained with $T$ tokens (total compute $C \approx 6NT$ FLOPs), we seek to predict the representation quality $Q(\ell)$ at layer $\ell$ for a downstream classification task.

\paragraph{Quality Metric.}
We use $k$-nearest-neighbor accuracy ($k=20$) on L2-normalized last-token representations, chance-normalized as:
\begin{equation}
    Q_{\text{norm}} = \frac{Q_{\text{kNN}} - 1/K}{1 - 1/K}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the number of classes. This removes task difficulty from the metric.

\subsection{The Universal Law}

We model quality in logit space as a Gaussian function of normalized depth $x = \ell / L$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:law}
    \text{logit}(Q_{\text{norm}}) = b_d + \alpha \log \frac{C}{N} - \beta \left(x - \mu\!\left(\log \frac{C}{N}\right)\right)^2
\end{equation}
where $\mu(r) = \mu_0 + \mu_1 r$ is the depth of peak quality, which depends on the compute-to-parameter ratio $r = \log(C/N)$.

The model has 4 \emph{physics parameters}: $\alpha$ (quality improvement rate with compute), $\beta$ (sharpness of the depth profile), $\mu_0, \mu_1$ (peak depth location and its dependence on training). Additionally, 4 \emph{dataset intercepts} $b_d$ capture task-specific baseline difficulty.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\alpha > 0$: more compute improves quality (analogous to scaling law exponent).
    \item $\beta > 0$: quality peaks at depth $\mu$ and falls off as a Gaussian, capturing the observation that representations improve through early/middle layers then degrade in late layers.
    \item $\mu_0 + \mu_1 r$: the optimal depth shifts with training, potentially explaining late-training degradation when $\mu < 1$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fitting Procedure}

We fit Equation~\ref{eq:law} by minimizing mean squared error using L-BFGS-B with 20 random restarts. Parameters are constrained to physically meaningful ranges (e.g., $\beta > 0$, $|\alpha| \leq 1$).

\subsection{Evaluation Datasets}

We use 4 hierarchical classification benchmarks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CLINC-150}: 150 intent classes, 10 domains
    \item \textbf{DBPedia Classes}: 68 fine-grained types, 9 categories
    \item \textbf{AG News}: 18 sub-categories, 4 topics
    \item \textbf{TREC}: 42 fine-grained types, 6 coarse categories
\end{itemize}

For each model and layer, we extract representations for up to 2000 test samples and compute kNN accuracy ($k=20$).

All folds achieve $R^2 > 0.73$ and are highly significant. The nested $F$-test confirms that our 4 physics parameters add substantial explanatory power beyond dataset-specific intercepts (all $p < 10^{-16}$).

\paragraph{Shape Universality.}
The Spearman rank correlation between predicted and observed depth profiles averages $\rho = 0.84$ across all (model, dataset, checkpoint) groups, with 84\% of profiles exceeding $\rho > 0.7$.

\subsection{Parameter Stability}

The physics parameters are remarkably stable across LOFO folds:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\alpha = 0.131 \pm 0.024$: consistent positive quality scaling with compute
    \item $\beta = 0.665 \pm 0.104$: moderate depth-profile sharpness
    \item $\mu_0 = -1.01 \pm 0.32$: peak depth location baseline
    \item $\mu_1 = 0.086 \pm 0.018$: peak shifts toward later layers with more training
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baseline Comparison}

Within a single family (Pythia), a nonparametric kNN baseline ($R^2 = 0.91$) outperforms our Gaussian law ($R^2 = 0.81$). However, kNN requires target-family data and cannot generalize cross-family. Our law's value lies in its \emph{transferability}: 8 parameters fitted on one family predict others with no additional fitting.

\section{Blind Test: BLOOM}
\label{sec:bloom}

BLOOM~\citep{workshop2022bloom} uses ALiBi positional encoding (vs.\ RoPE in Pythia/OLMo-2), a 250K multilingual tokenizer, and was trained on ROOTS (multilingual) rather than the Pile (English). We treat BLOOM as a truly blind test.

\paragraph{Results.}
Using frozen Pythia parameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shape transfer}: $\rho = 0.784$ (universal shape preserved)
    \item \textbf{Absolute prediction}: $R^2 = -2.75$ (fails---quality level is systematically lower)
    \item \textbf{With b\_d calibration} (4 parameters, 1 checkpoint): $R^2 = 0.57$, MAE $= 0.046$
\end{itemize}

The $b_d$ shift of $\approx -0.75$ in logit space is consistent across datasets, indicating a systematic difference in representation quality due to multilingual training.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The physics parameters ($\alpha, \beta, \mu_0, \mu_1$) are architecture-independent and transfer even across positional encoding schemes and training corpora. Dataset intercepts $b_d$ absorb family-specific quality baselines and require minimal calibration.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our study covers only decoder-only models up to 1.7B parameters. Extending to encoder-only (BERT), encoder-decoder (T5), and larger models ($>$7B) remains future work. The practical utility of the law for exact layer selection is limited (near-zero recovery ratio), though the shape information may inform architectural decisions.

\paragraph{Comparison to Scaling Laws.}
Our law is analogous to neural scaling laws but operates on a different axis: depth rather than loss. While scaling laws predict aggregate performance, our law predicts the internal geometry of representations. The physics parameters may connect to deeper theoretical frameworks relating information flow, compression, and generalization.

\paragraph{Implications.}
The universality of late-training degradation suggests that the standard practice of using final-layer representations is suboptimal for classification tasks. This finding is consistent with recent observations in the probing literature and has practical implications for representation extraction in production systems.

\section{Conclusion}

We present a shape-universal law for depth-wise representation quality in decoder language models. An 8-parameter Gaussian-in-logit model achieves $R^2 = 0.78$ across 5 model families in leave-one-family-out cross-validation, with all $p < 10^{-16}$. A blind test on a 6th family (BLOOM) confirms shape universality ($\rho = 0.78$) while showing that absolute quality levels require minimal family calibration. The discovery of universal late-training degradation across all 6 architecturally diverse families provides evidence for architecture-independent regularities in representation geometry, analogous to scaling laws for loss.
