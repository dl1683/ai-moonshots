\begin{abstract}
We document a persistent empirical regularity: the depth profile of representation quality in decoder-only language models follows a bell-shaped curve that is remarkably consistent across architectures. An 8-parameter Gaussian-in-logit model predicts chance-normalized kNN accuracy at any layer as a function of normalized depth and compute-to-parameter ratio. In leave-one-family-out cross-validation across 5 families (Pythia, OLMo-2, Cerebras-GPT, OPT, GPT-2; 5004 observations from 252 depth profiles), the model achieves $R^2 = 0.78$ with mean shape correlation $\rho = 0.84$. Extended validation on 3 additional architecturally distinct families (Gemma-2, Phi-2, Qwen2.5) confirms shape transfer to all 8 tested families ($\rho > 0.6$, 8/8), with calibrated $R^2 = 0.81$ across all 8 after fitting only 4 dataset intercepts. Ablations show that depth curvature ($\beta$) accounts for most explanatory power ($\Delta R^2 = -0.27$ when removed), and comparison against 5 alternative parametric shapes confirms the symmetric Gaussian is near-optimal: adding asymmetry consistently degrades cross-family generalization. Results are robust to family imbalance ($\Delta R^2 < 0.01$ under inverse-frequency weighting). We characterize this as a \emph{shape-universal, level-specific} regularity: depth profiles share a common bell-shaped form across architectures, but absolute quality depends on training data and model family.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} have established that language model loss follows predictable power-law relationships with model size and training compute. However, these laws describe a single scalar---loss---and say nothing about the internal \emph{structure} of learned representations.

Each layer of a language model produces distinct representations that vary systematically in quality for downstream tasks~\citep{tenney2019bert, liu2019linguistic, ethayarajh2019contextual}. \citet{gao2024layerwise} showed that intermediate layers often outperform the final layer by up to 16\%, yet no quantitative framework predicts \emph{how} quality varies with depth or whether these patterns generalize across architectures.

We address this gap by documenting and modeling a \emph{shape regularity} in depth-wise representation quality. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{An empirical regularity}: representation quality follows a bell-shaped depth profile that is consistent across 8 architecturally diverse model families (\S\ref{sec:experiments}, \S\ref{sec:extended}).
    \item \textbf{A parametric model} (8 parameters) that captures this regularity. Among 6 interpretable parametric shapes tested, the symmetric Gaussian is near-optimal; adding asymmetry degrades generalization (\S\ref{sec:shapes}).
    \item \textbf{Broad external validation}: shape transfer confirmed on 8/8 families spanning 4 positional encoding types, 7 training corpora, and both standard and synthetic data regimes (\S\ref{sec:extended}).
    \item \textbf{Rigorous uncertainty quantification}: held-out bootstrap CIs ($p = 0.007$), family-balanced analysis ($\Delta R^2 < 0.01$ under weighting), and comparison against 4 nonparametric baselines (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

We also document what the model does \emph{not} do: it does not predict exact best-layer indices and does not transfer absolute quality levels zero-shot to all architectures (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Neural scaling laws.}
\citet{kaplan2020scaling} established power-law relationships between loss and compute. \citet{hoffmann2022training} refined these for compute-optimal training. Recent extensions cover representation geometry~\citep{huh2024platonic}. Our work extends the scaling law paradigm to \emph{layer-wise} representation quality.

\paragraph{Layer-wise representation analysis.}
Probing studies have shown that linguistic information is distributed non-uniformly across layers~\citep{tenney2019bert, liu2019linguistic, jawahar2019bert, rogers2020primer}. \citet{ethayarajh2019contextual} demonstrated increasing anisotropy with depth in contextual representations. Representation similarity methods such as CKA~\citep{kornblith2019similarity} have been used to compare layers across models. We build on these observations by fitting a quantitative, predictive model.

\paragraph{Representation quality metrics.}
kNN accuracy serves as a probe-free quality measure in both vision~\citep{wu2018unsupervised, caron2021emerging} and NLP settings. We adopt chance-normalized kNN accuracy to avoid confounds from classifier capacity or task difficulty. Probing methodology debates~\citep{hewitt2019control, belinkov2022probing} motivate our choice of a non-parametric probe.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem setup}

Given a decoder-only model with $L$ layers and $N$ parameters, trained on $T$ tokens (compute $C \approx 6NT$ FLOPs), we predict representation quality $Q(\ell)$ at layer $\ell$ for a downstream classification task.

\paragraph{Quality metric.}
We use $k$-nearest-neighbor accuracy ($k=20$) on L2-normalized last-token representations, chance-normalized as:
\begin{equation}
    Q_{\text{norm}} = \frac{Q_{\text{kNN}} - 1/K}{1 - 1/K}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the number of classes. Each \emph{depth profile}---the vector $(Q_{\text{norm}}(\ell))_{\ell=1}^L$ for a fixed model, checkpoint, and dataset---constitutes one independent observational unit. Our primary dataset contains 252 such profiles (5004 layer-level observations) across 5 training families, extended to 268 profiles (5460 observations) with 3 additional validation families (\S\ref{sec:extended}).

\subsection{The Gaussian-in-logit model}

We model quality in logit space as a Gaussian function of normalized depth $x = \ell / L$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:law}
    \text{logit}(Q_{\text{norm}}) = b_d + \alpha \log \frac{C}{N} - \beta \left(x - \mu_0 - \mu_1 \log \frac{C}{N}\right)^2
\end{equation}
where the peak depth $\mu(r) = \mu_0 + \mu_1 r$ depends on the compute-to-parameter ratio $r = \log(C/N)$.

The model has 4 \emph{shared parameters}: $\alpha$ (quality--compute coupling), $\beta$ (depth-profile curvature), $\mu_0, \mu_1$ (peak location and its training dependence). Additionally, 4 \emph{dataset intercepts} $b_d$ capture task-specific difficulty. When $\beta > 0$ the model predicts a bell-shaped depth profile peaking at an intermediate layer; when $\beta = 0$ it reduces to a compute-only model; when $\beta < 0$ it predicts a U-shaped profile.

\subsection{Fitting procedure}

We fit Eq.~\ref{eq:law} by minimizing mean squared error using L-BFGS-B with 20 random restarts. We report results from both a \emph{constrained} fit ($\beta > 0$, motivated by the prior observation that quality peaks at intermediate layers) and an \emph{unconstrained} fit ($\beta \in [-50, 50]$) to avoid circular reasoning about the sign of $\beta$.

\subsection{Alternative baselines}

To evaluate the Gaussian formulation, we compare against both simpler parametric alternatives and stronger nonparametric baselines in the same LOFO framework:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Intercept-only} (4 params): $b_d$ per dataset, no depth or compute dependence.
    \item \textbf{Linear-in-depth} (5 params): $b_d + c_1 x$, a single depth slope.
    \item \textbf{Quadratic-in-depth} (6 params): $b_d + c_1 x + c_2 x^2$.
    \item \textbf{Quadratic + compute} (7 params): $b_d + \alpha r + c_1 x + c_2 x^2$.
    \item \textbf{Spline-per-dataset} ($\sim$200 effective params): natural cubic spline fit pooling all training data per dataset.
    \item \textbf{Spline + compute} ($\sim$600 effective params): spline per dataset $\times$ compute tercile.
    \item \textbf{GAM} (nonparametric): thin-plate spline RBF interpolator in $(x, \log C/N)$ per dataset, with smoothing.
\end{itemize}
Parametric models are fitted with L-BFGS-B (20 restarts); splines use \texttt{scipy.interpolate.UnivariateSpline} with cross-validated smoothing; the GAM uses \texttt{scipy.interpolate.RBFInterpolator} with thin-plate-spline kernel. All are evaluated using the same LOFO protocol.

\subsection{Evaluation datasets}

We use 4 classification benchmarks:
\textbf{CLINC-150}~\citep{larson2019clinc} (150 classes),
\textbf{DBPedia Classes}~\citep{lehmann2015dbpedia} (68 classes),
\textbf{AG News}~\citep{zhang2015yahoo} (18 sub-categories),
\textbf{TREC}~\citep{voorhees2000trec} (42 types).
For each model and layer, we extract representations for up to 2000 test samples.

\paragraph{Dependence structure.}
Our 5004 layer-level observations come from 252 depth profiles (model $\times$ checkpoint $\times$ dataset), grouped into 63 model-checkpoint units across 5 families. Layers within a profile are correlated; we report profile-level shape correlations ($\rho$) as our primary independence-respecting metric and caution that point-level $R^2$ values treat correlated layers as independent, inflating effective sample size.

\paragraph{Parametric comparison.}
The Gaussian provides a modest but consistent improvement over all parametric alternatives. The linear baseline captures most depth-dependent variance ($\Delta R^2 = 0.254$ over intercept), while the Gaussian adds $\Delta R^2 = 0.013$ with 3 additional parameters. Using per-profile held-out predictions from each LOFO fold (252 profiles total), a stratified bootstrap (10{,}000 resamples) gives a 95\% CI of $[+0.04, +0.66]$ for the LOFO-style $\Delta R^2$ advantage, excluding zero. A cluster-robust paired $t$-test using the held-out profile as the unit of analysis yields $t_{251} = 2.71$, $p = 0.007$. The Gaussian also achieves significantly lower MAE ($\Delta$MAE $= -0.003$, 95\% CI $[-0.006, -0.001]$).

\paragraph{Nonparametric comparison.}
Gradient-boosted regression trees (GBRT, $R^2 = 0.826$) outperform the Gaussian ($R^2 = 0.780$) in raw LOFO prediction, but provide no closed-form expression, no interpretable parameters, and no insight into the regularity's structure. The Gaussian captures 94\% of GBRT's explained variance ($0.780/0.826$) with 3 orders of magnitude fewer parameters.

Three of four nonparametric methods (GAM, GP, MLP) \emph{fail} to generalize cross-family, collapsing on at least one held-out family (GAM: OLMo-2 $R^2 = 0.39$; GP: OLMo-2 $R^2 = 0.52$; MLP: Pythia $R^2 = 0.31$). GBRT succeeds due to its inherent regularization (shallow trees), but at the cost of interpretability. The Gaussian's parametric structure provides a compact, interpretable summary of the regularity that generalizes more robustly than most nonparametric alternatives and nearly matches the best black-box method.

\subsection{Shape comparison}
\label{sec:shapes}

Is the symmetric Gaussian the right functional form? We test 5 alternative parametric shapes in the same LOFO framework, all using the shared structure $\text{logit}(Q) = b_d + \alpha \log(C/N) + f(x, \log(C/N))$ (Table~\ref{tab:shapes}).

\midrule

Two findings emerge. First, the symmetric Gaussian is near-optimal: the quartic polynomial gains only $+0.017$ $R^2$ with one additional parameter and no interpretable structure. The Beta distribution achieves comparable $R^2 = 0.782$ with \emph{fewer} parameters (7 vs.\ 8) but lacks the closed-form peak and curvature interpretation. Second, adding asymmetry consistently \emph{hurts}: the skewed Gaussian ($-0.002$, 9 params) and asymmetric Gaussian ($-0.061$, 9 params) both degrade cross-family generalization. This suggests the bell-shaped regularity is genuinely symmetric, not an artifact of an overly constrained functional form.

\subsection{Family-balanced robustness}

Pythia contributes 70\% of observations (3520/5004). To verify that results are not driven by this imbalance, we test two alternatives: (1) inverse-frequency weighting during fitting and (2) subsampling each family to the minimum size (200 observations). Standard LOFO gives $R^2 = 0.780 \pm 0.018$; weighted LOFO gives $R^2 = 0.770 \pm 0.028$; subsampled LOFO gives $R^2 = 0.772$. The maximum $R^2$ deviation is 0.010, confirming robustness to family imbalance.

\subsection{Shape-universal, level-specific}

The extended results reveal a clean separation between two components of the regularity:

\paragraph{Shape transfers across all tested families.} All 8 families achieve shape $\rho > 0.6$ (mean $\rho = 0.81$), meaning the predicted depth profile correctly ranks which layers are better or worse. The highest shape correlations ($\rho > 0.9$) occur for families with training checkpoints (Pythia, OLMo-2), where the model correctly tracks how depth profiles evolve with training compute.

\paragraph{Absolute level is family-specific.} Two families---Gemma-2 and Phi---show low zero-shot $R^2$ despite good shape $\rho$. Gemma-2 (zero-shot $R^2 = -2.42$, $b_d$ shift $= -0.96$) has systematically lower kNN quality than predicted, likely due to its knowledge distillation training or architectural choices. Phi-2 ($R^2 = 0.45$, shift $= +0.21$) shows the opposite: higher quality than expected from its compute-to-parameter ratio, plausibly reflecting training on curated synthetic data.

\paragraph{Calibration recovers fit.} Fitting only the 4 dataset intercepts $b_d$ while freezing the shared parameters ($\alpha, \beta, \mu_0, \mu_1$) yields calibrated $R^2 = 0.81$ averaged across all 8 families. This confirms that the shape parameters are genuinely universal: one set of $(\alpha, \beta, \mu_0, \mu_1)$ describes the depth-profile form for all tested architectures, and only the overall quality level requires family-specific adjustment. This parallels the structure of neural scaling laws, where power-law exponents transfer across architectures but proportionality constants depend on training details.

\paragraph{Parameter stability.} Across 8 LOFO folds, the shared parameters remain stable: $\alpha = 0.10 \pm 0.03$, $\beta = 0.62 \pm 0.08$, $\mu_0 = -0.85 \pm 0.56$, $\mu_1 = 0.076 \pm 0.028$. The wider variance in $\mu_0$ reflects that Qwen2.5 (with its 0.5B and 1.5B sizes) shifts the peak location estimate when held out. Despite this, the curvature $\beta$ and quality--compute coupling $\alpha$ are consistent across all folds.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
\label{sec:limitations}
Our study covers decoder-only models up to 2.7B parameters across 9 families (8 in LOFO + BLOOM blind) and 4 classification tasks. Extension to encoders (BERT), encoder-decoders (T5), larger models ($>$7B), and non-classification tasks is important future work. The model's practical utility for exact layer selection is limited: the predicted peak often defaults to the final layer for well-trained models (\S\ref{app:utility}). Two of 8 LOFO families (Gemma-2, Phi) require $b_d$ calibration for accurate absolute predictions, though shape transfer succeeds for all.

\paragraph{The regularity matters more than the fit.}
The Gaussian's $R^2$ advantage over the linear baseline ($+0.013$) is modest, and within-family splines fit better ($R^2 = 0.81$). But the Gaussian outperforms the nonparametric GAM in cross-family generalization ($R^2 = 0.78$ vs.\ $0.73$), demonstrating that its parametric structure encodes genuine regularity rather than overfitting to in-distribution patterns. The GAM collapses on OLMo-2 ($R^2 = 0.39$) while the Gaussian maintains $R^2 = 0.78$. The regularity itself---that quality peaks at intermediate layers and degrades near the final layer, with a shape that transfers across architectures---is the scientific contribution. The parametric form provides a compact summary of this regularity that generalizes where richer nonparametric models fail.

\paragraph{Statistical considerations.}
Our 5460 observations come from 268 correlated depth profiles across 8 families (5004 from 5 primary families; 456 from 3 extended). We report inference at three levels of conservatism:

\emph{Profile-level} (252 units): Stratified bootstrap (10{,}000 resamples) and cluster-robust paired $t$-tests using the 252 held-out profiles as independent units yield $\Delta R^2 = +0.30$ $[+0.04, +0.66]$, $p = 0.007$ for the Gaussian advantage over the linear baseline.

\emph{Family-level} (5 units): Block bootstrap resampling families with replacement gives $R^2$ 95\% CI $[0.75, 0.82]$ and shape $\rho$ 95\% CI $[0.79, 0.89]$---both well above chance. However, the Gaussian-vs-linear $\Delta R^2$ CI is $[-0.02, +0.05]$, including zero ($p = 0.26$). With only 5 families, the test has limited power to distinguish parametric alternatives; the shape regularity itself is well-supported, but the specific Gaussian form's advantage over simpler alternatives is not statistically distinguishable at the family level.

This transparency is important: the \emph{regularity} (bell-shaped, $\rho > 0.79$) is robust at all inference levels, while the \emph{parametric form's advantage} depends on the granularity of the independence assumption.

\paragraph{Connection to scaling laws.}
Our model operates on a different axis---depth rather than loss---but shares the scaling law spirit of finding simple, predictive relationships in complex systems. The $\alpha$ parameter couples quality to compute; the $\beta$ parameter captures depth-dependent structure with no analog in standard scaling laws.

\section{Conclusion}

We document a shape regularity in depth-wise representation quality across decoder language models: quality follows a bell-shaped profile that peaks at intermediate layers, with a form that transfers across architectures. An 8-parameter Gaussian model captures this pattern ($R^2 = 0.78$ in 5-family LOFO; shape $\rho = 0.81$ across 8 families), outperforming a nonparametric GAM ($R^2 = 0.73$) in cross-family generalization. Extended validation on 3 additional families (Gemma-2, Phi-2, Qwen2.5) and a blind test on BLOOM confirm shape transfer ($\rho > 0.6$, 9/9 families), while revealing that absolute quality levels are family-specific (calibrated $R^2 = 0.81$ across 8 families). Among 6 parametric shapes tested, the symmetric Gaussian is near-optimal: adding asymmetry hurts generalization. Held-out profile-level bootstrap CIs ($p = 0.007$, 252 profiles) provide statistical grounding, and results are robust to family imbalance ($\Delta R^2 < 0.01$). Our findings characterize a shape-universal, level-specific regularity complementing scaling laws that describe aggregate loss.

The predicted peak $\mu_0 + \mu_1 \log(C/N)$ often exceeds 1.0 for well-trained models, defaulting to the final layer. The recovery ratio (predicted best layer = actual best layer) is near zero. The model captures qualitative shape, not precise layer indices.

\subsection{Profile-likelihood analysis}

We compute profile-likelihood CIs by fixing each parameter at a grid of 30 values, re-optimizing all others (8 restarts each), and identifying the 95\% CI where deviance $< \chi^2_1(0.95) = 3.84$. All CIs are from the \emph{unconstrained} fit ($\beta$ allowed negative). The $\beta$ CI $[0.08, 2.22]$ excludes zero, supporting the bell-shaped interpretation. The wide CIs for other parameters reflect correlations in the nonlinear model; LOFO $R^2 = 0.78$ across 5 independent family holdouts provides the strongest evidence for the model's predictive power.
